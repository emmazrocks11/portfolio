01. Background

Topic: Affordable Groceries

As a university student myself, I understand and experience the issues that rise when it comes to balancing education and living necessities. The topic that my team and I chose to tackle was the need for affordable groceries as a university student.

Problem Statement

Grocery shopping is an essential part of university student life, and can pose problems when it comes to managing financial constraints. University students have noticed that groceries have become more expensive, and having that on top of tuition and housing expenses affects their nutrition. According to CBC News, food prices in 2025 could increase by 5% (Heydari 2024). Existing research shows that food costs are on the rise, and in consequence affordability becomes an important factor when it comes to grocery shopping.

According to Investopedia, buying in bulk does decrease the cost per unit, but often leads to overconsumption and waste. As a result, students miss out on savings.

Our app addresses these challenges by providing a platform where users can find grocery deals, easily find others interested in splitting bulk groceries if needed, coordinate purchases, and handle payments seamlessly. By facilitating collaborative shopping, the app promotes financial savings, sustainability, and good grocery shopping habits.

02. User research

Interview logistics

Through a series of conducted interviews, three participants were asked to share their grocery shopping situation as a university student. The first aim was to understand different grocery situations and methods used by university students who shop for themselves. This includes their journey from start to finish, and—if they do—how they find affordable groceries, be it flyers, coupons, online or physical. The second aim was to identify what in their personal experience works, and what does not.

my affinity diagram

Habits and preferences

  • Most students shop at budget-friendly stores like No Frills, Walmart, and Superstore.
  • Some prefer price matching and using apps like Flipp or PC Optimum to find deals.
  • Shopping frequency varies (weekly or bi-weekly), often planned around meal prepping.

Transportation and accessibility

  • Many students rely on public transit (bus, ION) or walking, which can be inconvenient, especially in winter.
  • Some students avoid carrying heavy groceries and opt for online grocery services like Uber Eats or Instacart.

Cost and budget

  • Price increases are noticeable, making affordability a key concern.
  • Students actively budget and look for sales or use digital coupons.
  • Splitting grocery costs with roommates is a common strategy.

Meal planning and waste

  • Many students meal prep to save time and reduce grocery trips.
  • Some struggle with buying ingredients they don't use in time, leading to waste.
  • Having a tool to help plan meals based on deals could be beneficial.

03. Design process

a) Tentative requirements: Must, Should, Could, Won't

Before my team and I split off to sketch ideas like crazy, we sat down and listed out some tentative requirements. By utilizing the MoSCoW framework, we were able to set ourselves up on the same page before branching out.

Priority Feature Category & Requirements
Must

Location-based results

  • User can implement their current location
  • Stores and deals must be shown relative to the user's location
  • Distance between user and the store must be shown clearly with an address and measurement

Flyer Deals

  • Prices and discounts must be visible for each grocery product
  • Valid-until dates

Search and Filtering

  • Users can search up specific products, brands, and stores
  • Users can filter different grocery categories: product, meat, sauces, bakery etc
  • Users can sort prices from high to low, and vice versa

Price Comparison

  • Users can see different deals and prices from different stores regarding the same product
Should

Saved Preferences

  • Save frequently purchased products
  • Remember past orders and quick reorder buttons

Product Descriptions

  • High Quality product photos
  • Images of nutrition facts, ingredient lists, and other product descriptions
Could

Recipes

  • Users can add their own recipes for quick access to ingredients
  • Application can suggest different recipes with grocery items that are on sale
  • Application can suggest recipes that are similar to what the user knows
Won't

Health and Nutrition Suggestions

c) Merging and converging ideas (and issues start to arise)

Our team of 4 gathered together a week later to share and critque each other's sketches.

However, upon comparison, we realized that we had conflicting ideas regarding the specific purpose of the app. While we all agreed that our topic was affordable groceries, we had different approaches when it came to applying functionalities.

eg. while I focused on making a marketplace app like Kijiji to split bulk groceries, another teammate aimed for shared grocery lists and store-based bulk purchases.

The issue with marketplace

Marketplace apps like Kijiji don't resemble physical grocery shopping experiences at all. Instead of going around "store to store" and purchasing items individually, users are familiar with going to a single store and purchasing items in all at once.

Store location

One member of the team created a sketch of a map feature that would show nearby listings to split bulk groceries. We ended up using this functionality to easily find stores so multiple people could purchase at once.

Shared grocery list

The addition of a shared grocery list became the core of our product. Having a single platform for users to coordinate purchases changed splitting bulk orders to be intuitive and convinient.

Prioritizing social features

We realized that there was a need to centralized the social features on our app. Users expressed that their grocery and eating habits were deeply rooted in routine and social interactions. A new icon button was added to the bottom navigation for easy access to finding, adding, and texting freinds.

d) Branding

Our team wanted to create a product that embodied fresh, fun, and social. Since our topic was affordable groceries, we strayed away from harsh cool colors, and gravitated towards oranges, yellows, and greens.

I was hunched over my laptop, trying to come up with a name for the app, and nothing was sounding right. Splitly? Grocery Split?... split... banana split...BananaSplit!

04. User testing and feedback

After creating the first iteration of the hi-fi prototype, we presented our product to 6 groups of 4-5 people. Each user was given a 2 minute presenation outlining the purpose, features, and design of the app.

Click to view presentation

User feedback

Here's some of the feedback we received from the users and how we addressed it:

1. Add search bar to find specific grocery lists

2. Add notification center on homepage and label urgent messages

3. Give the option to close chat aside from toggling the menu bar

4. Indicate which page the user is on using the menu bar icons

4. Make the unread messages more noticeable and different from read messages

5. Include a back button the search pages aside from toggling the menu bar

6. Toggle back and forth between map and list view (rather than just map view button)

7. Have a clear confirm payment page to add another payment checkpoint for users

05. Final prototype

After implementing the feedback, our team wrapped up the Figma prorotype and wrote a final design rationale on our product. Test out the final hi-fi prototype!
